# Jail and Imprisonment

**Summary:** Jails were seen as literal and spiritual cages; magical practices surrounding them focused on evasion, causing supernatural prison breaks, and utilizing mundane objects like brooms to either send enemies to jail or free them.

## Elements of the Account
- **Key figures involved:** Jailers, prisoners, rootworkers (notably Doctor Buzzard), and Saint Peter.
- **Methods used (legal or magical):** Shape-shifting or disappearing from locked cells, using brooms as tools of imprisonment, and placing protective charms to avoid capture.
- **Outcomes:** Prisoners miraculously disappearing from cells, enemies being cursed into incarceration, and the strategic use of jailers as unwitting participants in spellcraft.

## The Narrative / Practice
1. **The Broom as a Jail Tool:** Sweeping a broom over a person's foot, or intentionally striking them with it three times, was believed to curse them to land in jail within hours. Conversely, taking a new broom into a jail was thought to attract more prisoners, so much so that jail keepers would sometimes buy brooms from prisoners to keep the jail populated.
2. **Spiritual Evasion:** Advanced practitioners, such as the legendary Doctor Buzzard, were reputed to possess the ability to escape locked cells entirely. In one account, a man was locked in a cell by a jailer, but by morning, only a literal buzzard remained in the cell.
3. **Suspicion of Saints:** The physical reality of jail time caused some to reject traditional religious figures. One informant blamed her 30-day jail stint on working with Saint Peter, concluding that since he was an authority figure who fought and carried a sword, he must act like "the law."

## Lore & Context
**[CERNUNNOS.MD COMMENTARY]**
A cage is an illusion to a mind that understands its own boundlessness. The story of the buzzard in the locked cell is not just folklore; it is an assertion that the physical constraints of the state cannot hold true magical authority. The animal familiars and shapeshifting are a mockery of the iron bars.

**[ANTIGRAVITY.MD COMMENTARY]**
The pervasive fear of sudden imprisonment is woven deeply into these practices. The belief that simply sweeping a broom over someone's foot could land them in jail reflects the harsh reality of how easily and arbitrarily members of this community could be incarcerated. Distrust even extended to the Saints; an authority figure in heaven was viewed with the same suspicion as an authority figure on the street.
