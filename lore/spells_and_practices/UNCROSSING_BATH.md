# Uncrossing Bath

**Purpose:** A ritual cleansing to break a hex, remove a "crossed" condition, and wash away "tricked" or unnatural illnesses from the body.

## Components
- 9 teaspoons of cooking salt
- 1 dime's worth (a pinch) of saltpeter
- A pinch of sulfur (brimstone)
- 8 quarts of hot water (or a bathtub full)
- A running stream or river (for disposal)

## The Ritual

### Method 1: The Nine-Fold Cleansing
1. Fill a basin or tub with the hot water. Add the salt, saltpeter, and sulfur, stirring well.
2. Remove all clothing. Use the water to bathe yourself exactly nine times. 
3. **Crucial Motion:** Never rub upwards. Always start from the face or head and rub downwards towards your feet. This motion "washes down" the condition, pushing the illness out of your extremities and into the water.
4. Save the bathwater. Take it to a crossroads or a running stream, throw it over your left shoulder, and walk away without looking back.

### Method 2: The Running Stream Disposal
1. Boil saltpeter, sulfur, and salt in a pot of water. Let it cool enough to bathe with.
2. Bathe downwards, pushing the spell off your body.
3. Take the remaining dirty water to a river or a swift-running stream. Throw it near the middle of the channel. The running water carries the spell far away so it can never return.

## Lore & Context
> *"In the spiritual mechanics of Hoodoo, gravity is literal. A curse sits on the head and shoulders, and it must be dragged down and out through the soles of the feet. You cannot pull a crossed condition upward—you must let it fall. The running water is the great equalizer, a natural force that swallows the unnatural."* — **ANTIGRAVITY**

The "Uncrossing Bath" is the standard remedy for when a person has been "tricked in the skin" (cursed via contact with a dressed object or footprint powder). The salt purifies, the saltpeter provides explosive clearing energy, and the sulfur banishes the entity or intent behind the spell. The disposal of the bathwater is just as important as the bath itself; the illness is now in the water, and if not disposed of properly, it can re-infect the practitioner or infect someone else.
